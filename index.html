1 Determine the types of isomers present in the following compounds: [Cr(en)₂Cl₂]⁺, [Cr(NH₃)₄Cl₂]⁺, [Co(en)₂(NO₂)₂]⁺, [Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂] ▼
Answer

These compounds show different types of isomerism. Let's look at each:

[Cr(en)₂Cl₂]⁺ — Shows Geometric isomerism (cis & trans) and Optical isomerism.

  • In cis form: both Cl⁻ are on the same side → non-superimposable mirror images exist → optically active (d and l forms).
  • In trans form: Cl⁻ are opposite → has a plane of symmetry → optically inactive (meso form).

[Cr(NH₃)₄Cl₂]⁺ — Shows Geometric isomerism only.

  • cis: two Cl on same side of the octahedron.
  • trans: two Cl on opposite sides of the octahedron.
  • No optical isomers because NH₃ and Cl are simple, monodentate ligands; cis form here has a plane of symmetry.

[Co(en)₂(NO₂)₂]⁺ — Shows Geometric isomerism, Optical isomerism, and Linkage isomerism.

  • Geometric: cis (NO₂ on same side) and trans (NO₂ on opposite sides).
  • Optical: cis isomer is optically active.
  • Linkage isomerism: NO₂⁻ can bind via N (as nitro, –NO₂) or via O (as nitrito, –ONO).

[Pt(NH₃)₂Cl₂] — This is a square planar complex. Shows Geometric isomerism only.

  • cis: Both Cl on the same side (used as the anticancer drug cisplatin).
  • trans: Cl on opposite corners of the square.
  • No optical isomers — both cis and trans forms have planes of symmetry.

4 What do you mean by chelating ligand? Give example. ▼
Answer

A chelating ligand (from Greek word "chele" meaning claw) is a ligand that has two or more donor atoms in its structure and can simultaneously bind to the same metal ion at multiple points, forming a ring-like structure called a chelate ring.

The complex formed is called a chelate complex, and the process is called chelation.

Why chelates are stable? Because multiple bonds hold the ligand to the metal simultaneously. Breaking all bonds at once is very difficult — this is called the chelate effect.

Examples:

  • Ethylenediamine (en), H₂N–CH₂–CH₂–NH₂ — bidentate (2 donor N atoms). Example: [Cu(en)₂]²⁺
  • Oxalate ion (ox²⁻), ⁻OOC–COO⁻ — bidentate (2 donor O atoms).
  • EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetate) — hexadentate (6 donor atoms: 2 N + 4 O). Used in medicines and water treatment.
  • Acetylacetonate (acac⁻) — bidentate (2 O atoms).

8 The magnetic moment of [Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ is 5.9 B.M. and [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻ is 1.73 B.M. — Explain with CFT. ▼
Answer

Both complexes contain Fe³⁺ (d⁵), but they show very different magnetic moments. The key is the strength of the ligand field.

[Fe(H₂O)₆]³⁺ — μ = 5.9 B.M. (n = 5 unpaired electrons)

H₂O is a weak field ligand. Δo (crystal field splitting energy) is small, less than pairing energy (P).

Weak field: Δo < P → High spin complex d⁵ configuration: t₂g³ eg² All 5 electrons remain unpaired (Hund's rule wins) μ = √[5(5+2)] = √35 ≈ 5.92 B.M.

[Fe(CN)₆]³⁻ — μ = 1.73 B.M. (n = 1 unpaired electron)

CN⁻ is a strong field ligand. Δo is very large, greater than pairing energy (P).

Strong field: Δo > P → Low spin complex d⁵ configuration: t₂g⁵ eg⁰ 4 electrons are paired, only 1 remains unpaired μ = √[1(1+2)] = √3 ≈ 1.73 B.M.
Conclusion: The ligand field strength (position in spectrochemical series) determines whether a complex is high spin or low spin, directly affecting the magnetic moment.
9 Why does [Ti(H₂O)₆]³⁺ show red-violet colour? ▼
Answer

Ti³⁺ has a d¹ electronic configuration. In the octahedral complex [Ti(H₂O)₆]³⁺, the single d-electron occupies the lower energy t₂g level.

When white light falls on the complex: → The d-electron absorbs a photon of specific energy → It jumps from t₂g level to eg level → This transition is called a d-d transition

The complex absorbs light in the green-yellow region (~500 nm). The light that is transmitted (not absorbed) is the complementary colour, which is red-violet.

What we see = complementary colour of absorbed light.
Absorbed: green-yellow → Transmitted: red-violet (what our eye sees).

This is a perfect example of a single d-d transition, making [Ti(H₂O)₆]³⁺ one of the simplest coloured coordination compounds to explain.

11 Why does KMnO₄ show deep violet colour? ▼
Answer

KMnO₄ contains Mn⁷⁺ ion, which has a d⁰ electronic configuration (zero d-electrons).

Since d⁰ means no electrons in d-orbitals, a d-d transition is impossible. However, KMnO₄ is intensely violet! Why?

The colour of KMnO₄ is due to Charge Transfer (CT) transition, not d-d transition.

Oxygen ligands donate electrons to the empty d-orbitals of Mn⁷⁺ when light is absorbed. This transfer of electron density from ligand (O) to metal (Mn) is a Ligand-to-Metal Charge Transfer (LMCT).

LMCT transitions are Laporte-allowed and very intense, resulting in the deep, highly saturated violet colour of KMnO₄, much more vivid than typical d-d transitions.

12 Why does K₂Cr₂O₇ show deep orange colour? ▼
Answer

K₂Cr₂O₇ (potassium dichromate) contains Cr⁶⁺, which has a d⁰ configuration — no d-electrons.

Just like KMnO₄, the colour cannot be due to d-d transitions. Instead, it is due to Ligand-to-Metal Charge Transfer (LMCT):

  • Oxygen ligands (O²⁻) have filled orbitals.
  • Cr⁶⁺ has empty d-orbitals.
  • Light absorption causes transfer of electron density from O → Cr.
  • This transition absorbs blue-violet light (~400–450 nm).
  • The transmitted/reflected complementary colour is deep orange.
The high oxidation state (+6) of Cr makes the LMCT very easy, leading to absorption in the visible region and thus the intense orange colour.

14 [NiCl₄]²⁻ is paramagnetic but [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻ is diamagnetic. Explain. ▼
Answer

Both contain Ni²⁺ (d⁸). The difference is due to the ligand field strength.

[NiCl₄]²⁻ — Cl⁻ is weak field ligand:

Geometry: Tetrahedral (sp³ hybridisation) d⁸ in tetrahedral field: e⁴ t₂⁴ → 2 unpaired electrons remain μ = √[2(2+2)] = √8 ≈ 2.83 B.M. → Paramagnetic

[Ni(CN)₄]²⁻ — CN⁻ is very strong field ligand:

CN⁻ forces pairing of all d electrons One 3d orbital becomes empty → hybridisation changes to dsp² Geometry: Square planar d⁸ fully paired → 0 unpaired electrons μ = 0 → Diamagnetic
Key: Strong field CN⁻ causes electrons to pair up, removing all unpaired electrons. Weak field Cl⁻ does not, leaving 2 unpaired electrons.
15 [NiCl₄]²⁻ is tetrahedral but [Ni(CN)₄]²⁻ is square planar. Explain. ▼
Answer

This is directly related to the answer above — the ligand field strength determines the geometry of Ni²⁺ (d⁸) complexes.

[NiCl₄]²⁻:

  • Cl⁻ is a weak field ligand → small crystal field splitting.
  • The energy cost of pairing electrons is not compensated by the crystal field stabilisation.
  • Ni²⁺ uses sp³ hybridisation → tetrahedral geometry, 2 unpaired electrons.

[Ni(CN)₄]²⁻:

  • CN⁻ is a very strong field ligand → large crystal field splitting energy.
  • Electrons pair up in 3d orbitals; one 3d orbital empties out.
  • Ni²⁺ uses dsp² hybridisation (using one empty 3d orbital) → square planar geometry, 0 unpaired electrons.
Rule: d⁸ metal ions — strong field ligands → square planar; weak field ligands → tetrahedral. Square planar is more stable when CFSE gain is large enough to pay for the extra pairing energy.
16 Calculate the magnetic moment and pairing energy of [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺. ▼
Answer

Co³⁺ has a d⁶ configuration. NH₃ is a strong field ligand.

Strong field → Low spin complex d⁶: t₂g⁶ eg⁰ Number of unpaired electrons (n) = 0 Magnetic moment: μ = √[n(n+2)] = √[0(0+2)] = 0 B.M.

[Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ is diamagnetic (μ = 0 B.M.)

Pairing energy: In a d⁶ low-spin complex, there are 3 pairs of electrons in t₂g.

Free ion d⁶ (high spin): t₂g⁴ eg² → 4 unpaired electrons → 1 pair forced Low spin d⁶: t₂g⁶ eg⁰ → 0 unpaired → 3 pairs forced total Extra pairs formed going from high spin to low spin = 2 extra pairs ∴ Extra pairing energy cost = 2P
The CFSE gain must be greater than 2P for low spin to be favoured. For Co³⁺ with NH₃, Δo ≫ 2P, so low spin is strongly preferred.
17 Calculate ΔO value for d⁵ octahedral high spin and low spin complex. ▼
Answer

For a d⁵ octahedral complex:

High Spin (weak field): t₂g³ eg²

CFSE = 3×(-0.4Δo) + 2×(+0.6Δo) = -1.2Δo + 1.2Δo = 0 Δo

CFSE = 0 Δo for high spin d⁵ — this is why Mn²⁺ and Fe³⁺ complexes with weak field ligands are colourless or have very pale colours — no crystal field stabilisation at all!

Low Spin (strong field): t₂g⁵ eg⁰

CFSE = 5×(-0.4Δo) + 0×(+0.6Δo) = -2.0Δo

CFSE = -2.0 Δo for low spin d⁵. Additionally, 2 extra electron pairs form, so there is an extra pairing energy cost of 2P. Net CFSE = -2.0Δo + 2P.

1State Ostwald's Dilution Law.▼
Answer

Ostwald's Dilution Law states that for a weak electrolyte, the degree of ionisation (α) increases on dilution. The dissociation constant (Ka) is related to concentration (C) and degree of ionisation (α) as:

For weak acid HA ⇌ H⁺ + A⁻ Ka = Cα² / (1 - α) When α is very small (≪ 1): Ka ≈ Cα² Therefore: α = √(Ka / C) ∴ As C decreases (dilution), α increases.

This means: the more dilute the solution, the greater the ionisation. However, the total number of ions may still decrease on dilution.

Limitation: This law applies only to weak electrolytes. It does not apply to strong electrolytes (like HCl, NaOH) which are considered completely ionised.
2Calculate degree of ionisation of 0.1 M CH₃COOH. Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵▼
Answer
Given: C = 0.1 M, Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵ Using Ostwald's approximation (α ≪ 1): α = √(Ka / C) = √(1.8×10⁻⁵ / 0.1) = √(1.8×10⁻⁴) = √(18×10⁻⁵) = 1.342×10⁻² × √10 ... = √(0.00018) = 0.01342 α ≈ 0.0134 = 1.34%
Answer: α ≈ 1.34% — Only about 1.34% of acetic acid ionises in 0.1 M solution.
3Calculate degree of ionisation and [H⁺] for 0.01 M formic acid. Ka = 2.1×10⁻⁴▼
Answer
Given: C = 0.01 M, Ka = 2.1×10⁻⁴ α = √(Ka / C) = √(2.1×10⁻⁴ / 0.01) = √(2.1×10⁻²) = √(0.021) = 0.1449 Check: α ≈ 0.145 (14.5%) — not negligible, so use exact formula: Ka = Cα²/(1-α) → α² × 0.01 / (1-α) = 2.1×10⁻⁴ Solving: 0.01α² + 2.1×10⁻⁴ α - 2.1×10⁻⁴ × 1 = 0 [wait, rearrange] Actually: Cα² + Ka·α - Ka = 0 0.01α² + 2.1×10⁻⁴α - 2.1×10⁻⁴ = 0 Using quadratic: α = [-2.1×10⁻⁴ + √((2.1×10⁻⁴)² + 4×0.01×2.1×10⁻⁴)] / (2×0.01) Discriminant = 4.41×10⁻⁸ + 8.4×10⁻⁶ = 8.444×10⁻⁶ √discriminant = 2.906×10⁻³ α = (-2.1×10⁻⁴ + 2.906×10⁻³) / 0.02 = 2.696×10⁻³ / 0.02 ≈ 0.1348 α ≈ 0.135 = 13.5% [H⁺] = Cα = 0.01 × 0.135 = 1.35×10⁻³ M

5Calculate pH of: (i) 10⁻⁹ M HCl   (ii) 10⁻⁸ M NaOH▼
Answer

These are very dilute solutions — we must account for water's own ionisation contribution!

(i) 10⁻⁹ M HCl: [H⁺] from HCl = 10⁻⁹ M (very small) [H⁺] from water = 10⁻⁷ M (dominant here) Total [H⁺] = 10⁻⁷ + 10⁻⁹ = 10⁻⁷(1 + 0.01) = 1.01×10⁻⁷ M pH = -log(1.01×10⁻⁷) ≈ 6.996 ≈ 7.0 (Cannot have pH < 7 for such a dilute acid — water's contribution dominates) (ii) 10⁻⁸ M NaOH: [OH⁻] from NaOH = 10⁻⁸ M [OH⁻] from water = 10⁻⁷ M Total [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷ + 10⁻⁸ = 1.1×10⁻⁷ M pOH = -log(1.1×10⁻⁷) = 7 - log(1.1) ≈ 7 - 0.041 = 6.959 pH = 14 - 6.959 = 7.041 ≈ 7.04
Key concept: At extreme dilutions, water's autoionisation (Kw = 10⁻¹⁴) contributes significantly to [H⁺] or [OH⁻]. The pH can never exceed 7 for an acid solution or go below 7 for a base solution.

15What is the ionic product of water?▼

Water undergoes autoionisation (self-ionisation):

The ionic product of water (Kw) is the product of the concentrations of H⁺ and OH⁻ ions in pure water (or any aqueous solution):

Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] At 25°C: [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷ mol/L (measured) Kw = 10⁻⁷ × 10⁻⁷ = 10⁻¹⁴ mol²/L²

Key points:

  • Kw increases with temperature (water ionisation is endothermic).
  • At 25°C, Kw = 10⁻¹⁴. At 37°C (body temperature), Kw ≈ 2.4×10⁻¹⁴.
  • pH + pOH = pKw = 14 (at 25°C).
  • In any aqueous solution, Kw is constant at constant temperature.

Mutarotation, anomeric carbon, Haworth projections, reactions of sugars, and chemical properties.
1What do you mean by mutarotation?▼
Answer

Mutarotation is the spontaneous change in the specific optical rotation of a freshly prepared solution of a carbohydrate over time, until it reaches an equilibrium value.

Why it happens: When a crystalline sugar like glucose is dissolved in water, the cyclic ring opens briefly (ring-chain tautomerism), and then re-closes to give either the α or β anomer in different proportions.

D-Glucose example: α-D-Glucopyranose ⇌ Open-chain form ⇌ β-D-Glucopyranose [α]D = +112.2° [α]D = +52.7° (open) [α]D = +18.7° Equilibrium mixture: ~36% α + ~64% β Final optical rotation at equilibrium: +52.7°

This change in optical rotation from initial to equilibrium value is called mutarotation. It is catalysed by acids, bases, and enzymes.

2What do you mean by anomeric carbon?▼
Answer

The anomeric carbon is the carbon atom that becomes a new chiral centre when a monosaccharide undergoes ring closure (cyclisation).

In glucose, the open-chain aldehyde carbon (C-1) reacts with the C-5 hydroxyl group to form a cyclic hemiacetal. C-1 now has four different groups attached → it becomes chiral → it is the anomeric carbon.

Two anomers arise from this:
• α-anomer: –OH at anomeric carbon is on the same side as the reference group (below the ring in Haworth projection for D-sugars).
• β-anomer: –OH at anomeric carbon is on the opposite side (above the ring for D-sugars).

The two anomers are diastereomers of each other and are called anomers. They interconvert through mutarotation via the open-chain form.

3Convert D-Ribose to D-Glucose.▼
Answer

D-Ribose is a pentose sugar (5 carbons). D-Glucose is a hexose (6 carbons). We use the Kiliani-Fischer synthesis to extend the chain.

  1. Step 1 — HCN addition (Kiliani): HCN adds to the aldehyde group of D-Ribose → forms a cyanohydrin. This creates two new C-6 epimers (since C-2 becomes a new chiral centre).
  2. Step 2 — Hydrolysis: The nitrile (CN) group is hydrolysed with H₂O/H⁺ → forms an α-hydroxy acid (aldonic acid) with 6 carbons.
  3. Step 3 — Lactonisation & Reduction: The acid is converted to its lactone (cyclic ester) and then reduced with Na-Hg amalgam in dilute H₂SO₄ → gives D-Glucose and D-Mannose (the two epimers at C-2).
D-Ribose + HCN → Cyanohydrin → Aldonic acid → Lactone → Na-Hg / H₂O → D-Glucose + D-Mannose
D-Glucose is isolated by specific crystallisation.

7Why is sucrose a non-reducing sugar?▼
Answer

Sucrose (table sugar) is made of glucose + fructose joined together. The key reason it cannot reduce is:

The glycosidic bond in sucrose is formed between the anomeric carbon of glucose (C-1) and the anomeric carbon of fructose (C-2). Both anomeric carbons are involved in bonding.

In a reducing sugar, the free anomeric –OH (free hemiacetal or hemiketal) can open the ring to give a free aldehyde or ketone group, which then reduces the reagent (Fehling's, Benedict's, or Tollens').

In sucrose, both anomeric carbons are locked in a glycosidic bond — neither can open the ring freely. So there is no free aldehyde/ketone group → sucrose cannot reduce Benedict's or Fehling's solution.

Sucrose is therefore a non-reducing disaccharide. Upon hydrolysis (acid or enzyme), it gives glucose + fructose, both of which are reducing sugars.

8Convert D-Glucose to D-Mannose.▼
Answer

D-Glucose and D-Mannose are C-2 epimers — they differ only in the configuration at C-2. The conversion is done by the Lobry de Bruyn–van Ekenstein rearrangement (isomerisation in dilute alkali) or via epimerisation.

Method via oxidation–reduction:

  1. Step 1 — Oxidise D-Glucose: With bromine water → D-Gluconic acid (aldonic acid, C-1 becomes COOH).
  2. Step 2 — Lactonise: Glucose → Gluconolactone.
  3. Step 3 — React with SOCl₂: Converts –OH at C-2 to –Cl (with inversion).
  4. Step 4 — Replace with –OH: With AgOH or NaOH → gives C-2 inverted sugar = D-Mannonic acid.
  5. Step 5 — Reduce: D-Mannonic acid → D-Mannose (using Na-Hg amalgam).
Simple way to remember: Glucose and Mannose differ only at C-2. In Fischer projection, –OH at C-2 is on the right in glucose, and on the left in mannose.
9Convert Glucose to Fructose.▼
Answer

Glucose (an aldose) → Fructose (a ketose). This interconversion happens in the body enzymatically, but chemically it can be done via the Lobry de Bruyn-van Ekenstein transformation.

Reaction: Dilute alkali (like Ca(OH)₂) in solution causes isomerisation of glucose through an enediol intermediate:

D-Glucose ——[dilute alkali]——→ Enediol intermediate ↓ D-Fructose + D-Mannose (mixture)

Mechanism:

  1. Base removes the C-2 proton from glucose → enolate forms.
  2. The enolate is the enediol intermediate (shared between glucose, fructose, mannose).
  3. Reprotonation at C-1 gives fructose; at C-2 gives mannose.
In the body, the enzyme phosphoglucose isomerase converts glucose-6-phosphate to fructose-6-phosphate in glycolysis with 100% specificity (no mannose formed).

11What is an Epimer? Give an example of a C-3 Epimer.▼
Answer

An epimer is a type of diastereomer that differs from another carbohydrate in the configuration at only one specific chiral carbon (not the anomeric carbon).

Epimers are related to each other specifically by the position where the –OH group is flipped.

Examples:
• C-2 epimers: D-Glucose and D-Mannose (differ at C-2 only)
• C-2 epimers: D-Glucose and D-Galactose... actually D-Galactose differs at C-4
• C-3 epimer: D-Ribose and D-Arabinose — they differ only at C-3. In D-Ribose, C-3 has –OH on the right; in D-Arabinose, C-3 has –OH on the left in Fischer projection.
• Another C-3 epimer pair: D-Allose and D-Altrose.

12Give two evidences in favour of the linear (open-chain) structure of D-glucose.▼
Answer

Evidence 1 — Reaction with 7 moles of Acetic Anhydride (Acetylation):

D-Glucose reacts with acetic anhydride [(CH₃CO)₂O] in the presence of sodium acetate to give glucose pentaacetate. This means glucose has 5 free –OH groups, all of which get acetylated. This is consistent with the open-chain structure that has 5 –OH groups (at C-2, C-3, C-4, C-5 and one aldehydic –OH equivalent).

Evidence 2 — Reduction to a hexahydric alcohol (Sorbitol):

When D-glucose is reduced with sodium amalgam (Na-Hg) in dilute acid, it gives Sorbitol, a hexahydric alcohol (6 –OH groups). This confirms that glucose has a straight chain of 6 carbons with the formula C₆H₁₂O₆ and a carbonyl group (since a hexahydric alcohol can only come from reducing all 6 carbons having one carbonyl + 5 hydroxyls).

Glucose (CHO group + 5 CHOH groups) + 2[H] → Sorbitol (6 CHOH groups)
Other supporting evidences: Reaction with HCN, hydroxylamine, phenylhydrazine, and oxidation with HNO₃ to saccharic acid — all consistent with the open-chain structure.
13Glucose and Fructose give the same osazone. Explain.▼
Answer

When glucose or fructose react with excess phenylhydrazine (C₆H₅NHNH₂), they both give the same product called glucosazone (a bright yellow crystalline compound).

Why the same osazone?

In osazone formation, 3 moles of phenylhydrazine react with the sugar. The reaction involves:

  1. C-1 (aldehyde of glucose / potential aldehyde of fructose) reacts with 1st phenylhydrazine → phenylhydrazone.
  2. Then C-2 is oxidised and reacts with 2nd phenylhydrazine.
  3. The final osazone has a –CH=N–NHPh group at both C-1 and C-2.
Since C-3, C-4, C-5 of both glucose and fructose have identical configurations, and the osazone reaction destroys and replaces C-1 and C-2, the resulting osazone is the same compound. The sugar's identity below C-3 is all that matters.

This also explains why D-Mannose gives the same osazone — Mannose differs from Glucose only at C-2, which is also destroyed in osazone formation.

16Prove that glucose contains one aldehyde group.▼

1What do you mean by Zwitter ion?▼
Answer

A Zwitter ion (also called dipolar ion or amphoteric ion, from German "Zwitter" meaning hybrid/hermaphrodite) is a molecule that carries both a positive charge and a negative charge simultaneously, but has no net charge overall.

In amino acids, this happens because:

  • The amino group (–NH₂) acts as a base and accepts a proton → becomes –NH₃⁺ (positively charged)
  • The carboxyl group (–COOH) acts as an acid and donates a proton → becomes –COO⁻ (negatively charged)
Normal form: H₂N–CH(R)–COOH Zwitter ion: ⁺H₃N–CH(R)–COO⁻
The zwitter ion form is the predominant form of amino acids in solid state and at their isoelectric point in solution. It's why amino acids have high melting points, are water-soluble, and don't act like simple amines or acids.
2What do you mean by isoelectric point?▼
Answer

The isoelectric point (pI or IEP) is the specific pH at which an amino acid (or protein) exists predominantly as a zwitter ion (equal number of positive and negative charges) and carries zero net electrical charge.

At the isoelectric point:

  • The amino acid does not migrate in an electric field during electrophoresis.
  • Solubility is at its minimum (because zwitter ions don't repel each other as much).
  • Viscosity is minimum.
For a simple amino acid (one –NH₂, one –COOH): pI = (pKa₁ + pKa₂) / 2 Where pKa₁ = pKa of –COOH group pKa₂ = pKa of –NH₃⁺ group

Examples: Glycine has pI = 5.97, Alanine has pI = 6.01, Glutamic acid (acidic) has pI = 3.22, Lysine (basic) has pI = 9.74.

14What do you mean by peptide bond?▼
Answer

A peptide bond is a special type of amide bond (–CO–NH–) formed when the α-carboxyl group (–COOH) of one amino acid reacts with the α-amino group (–NH₂) of another amino acid, with the loss of one water molecule.

H₂N–CHR₁–COOH + H₂N–CHR₂–COOH → H₂N–CHR₁–CO–NH–CHR₂–COOH + H₂O ↑ Peptide bond (–CO–NH–)

Key properties of a peptide bond:

  • It has partial double bond character (resonance between C=O and C–N) → restricted rotation around the C–N bond.
  • It is planar (trans configuration is preferred).
  • It is hydrolysed by strong acids, bases, or proteolytic enzymes.
  • It is detected by the biuret test (violet colour with CuSO₄ + NaOH).

A chain of amino acids connected by peptide bonds is called a polypeptide. Proteins are polypeptides.

Heterogeneous Equilibrium & Phase Rule
Gibbs phase rule, phase diagrams for H₂O, CO₂ and Sulphur, Clausius-Clapeyron equation, and system calculations.
1Define phase (P), component (C) and degree of freedom (F).▼
Answer

Phase (P): A phase is a physically distinct, homogeneous, and mechanically separable portion of a system that is uniform throughout in composition and physical state.

  • Example: Ice + Water + Water vapour = 3 phases (solid, liquid, gas)
  • All gases mix freely → gas mixtures = 1 phase
  • Miscible liquids → 1 phase; immiscible liquids → 2 phases

Component (C): The minimum number of independently variable chemical species needed to describe the composition of all phases in a system.

  • For pure water: C = 1 (only H₂O)
  • For NaCl solution: C = 2 (NaCl + H₂O)
  • For CaCO₃ ⇌ CaO + CO₂: C = 2 (any two of the three species fix the third)

Degree of Freedom (F): The minimum number of independent variables (temperature, pressure, concentration) that must be specified to completely and uniquely define the state of a system without causing any phase to appear or disappear.

Gibbs Phase Rule: F = C – P + 2 Where: F = degree of freedom C = number of components P = number of phases 2 = temperature and pressure (two variables)
2What is the 'triple point'? What is the degree of freedom at the triple point?▼
Answer

The triple point is a specific set of temperature and pressure at which all three phases of a substance (solid, liquid, and gas) coexist simultaneously in equilibrium.

For water: Triple point is at 273.16 K (0.01°C) and 4.58 mm Hg (0.006 atm).
For sulphur: Metastable triple point at 95.6°C and 0.006 mm Hg.

Degree of freedom at the triple point:

For water at triple point: C = 1 (only H₂O), P = 3 (solid + liquid + gas) F = C – P + 2 = 1 – 3 + 2 = 0 F = 0 → Invariant point (no freedom)

F = 0 means the triple point is a fixed point — you cannot change temperature or pressure without losing one of the three phases. This is why the triple point of water is used as a reference standard in thermometry.

3What is the eutectic point or cryohydric point?▼
Answer

The eutectic point (also called the cryohydric point when water is one component) is the specific temperature and composition at which a liquid mixture solidifies completely at a constant temperature to give a mixture of two solid phases simultaneously.

Key features of the eutectic point:
• The eutectic mixture has the lowest possible melting point for that two-component system.
• At this point: P = 3 (two solids + one liquid), C = 2 → F = 2 – 3 + 2 = 1... wait: since the eutectic solidifies at a fixed T and P, it appears to have F = 0 if pressure is fixed. At constant P: F = C – P + 1 = 2 – 3 + 1 = 0 → invariant.
• The eutectic mixture behaves like a pure substance in that it has a sharp melting point.

Example: Pb-Ag system: Eutectic point at 303°C, with 97.4% Pb and 2.6% Ag. Below this temperature, both Pb and Ag crystallise simultaneously.

Practical uses: Soldering (Sn-Pb eutectic at 183°C), antifreeze mixtures, and identifying precious metals (Pattinson's process uses eutectic behaviour to concentrate silver from lead).

4What is reduced phase rule?▼
Answer

When pressure is kept constant (as in most laboratory experiments, where the system is open to the atmosphere), the phase rule is modified. The pressure term is no longer a variable, so we lose one degree of freedom.

This modified form is called the Reduced Phase Rule or Condensed Phase Rule:

Normal Phase Rule: F = C – P + 2 Reduced Phase Rule: F' = C – P + 1 (When pressure is fixed, the '2' becomes '1')

This is especially useful in studying two-component systems (binary alloys, salt-water systems) where experiments are done at atmospheric pressure. The degree of freedom calculated is only with respect to temperature and composition (not pressure).

Example: Pb-Ag system at fixed pressure. At eutectic point: F' = 2 – 3 + 1 = 0 (invariant). On a cooling curve, eutectic solidification appears as a horizontal halt.
5What is peritectic/transition temperature? What is its value for sulphur?▼
Answer

The transition temperature (or peritectic temperature) is the temperature at which one solid phase converts reversibly into another solid phase — i.e., two allotropic (or polymorphic) forms of a substance are in equilibrium.

At the transition temperature:

  • The two solid forms coexist in equilibrium.
  • If temperature rises above it → one form becomes stable; below → the other is stable.
For Sulphur:
Transition temperature = 95.6°C (368.55 K) at 1 atm
Below 95.6°C: Rhombic sulphur (α-S) is stable.
Above 95.6°C (up to melting point 119°C): Monoclinic sulphur (β-S) is stable.
At exactly 95.6°C: Both rhombic and monoclinic sulphur coexist in equilibrium.

Peritectic temperature specifically refers to a temperature in two-component systems where a solid phase reacts with a liquid phase to form a different solid phase. Example: In the Na₂SO₄–H₂O system, Na₂SO₄·10H₂O converts to anhydrous Na₂SO₄ at 32.4°C (transition temperature).

7Write down the conditions for phase equilibrium.▼
Answer

For a system to be in phase equilibrium, the following thermodynamic conditions must be satisfied simultaneously for all phases:

  1. Thermal Equilibrium: Temperature must be equal in all phases.
    Tα = Tβ = Tγ = ...
    (If temperatures differ, heat flows until they equalise.)
  2. Mechanical Equilibrium: Pressure must be equal in all phases.
    Pα = Pβ = Pγ = ...
    (If pressures differ, phases expand/contract.)
  3. Chemical Equilibrium (Chemical Potential): The chemical potential (μ) of each component must be equal in all phases.
    μᵢα = μᵢβ = μᵢγ = ... for each component i.
    (If chemical potentials differ, mass transfers between phases.)
Summary: Phase equilibrium requires that T, P, and chemical potential (μ) of every component are uniform throughout all phases. The chemical potential condition is the most fundamental — it accounts for concentration-driven mass transfer between phases.
8Establish the Gibbs phase rule relationship.▼
Answer

Derivation of Gibbs Phase Rule: F = C – P + 2

1Consider a system with C components and P phases in equilibrium.
2Total variables: To describe a single phase, we need (C–1) concentration terms (since concentrations must add to 1), plus T and P. For P phases: Total variables = P(C–1) + 2.
3Equilibrium conditions (constraints): For each component, its chemical potential must be equal in all phases. This gives (P–1) equations per component. Total equations = C(P–1).
4Degree of freedom = Variables – Constraints:
F = [P(C-1) + 2] - [C(P-1)] = PC - P + 2 - CP + C = C - P + 2 ∴ F = C - P + 2
Gibbs Phase Rule: F = C – P + 2
This elegant equation was derived by J.W. Gibbs (1875) and is one of the most powerful tools in physical chemistry for predicting the behaviour of multi-phase systems.
9Why can a system not have four phases?▼
Answer

For a one-component system (C = 1), the phase rule gives:

F = C – P + 2 = 1 – P + 2 = 3 – P For P = 4: F = 3 – 4 = –1 A negative degree of freedom is physically impossible.

Since F cannot be negative (you can't have fewer than zero variables to specify), a system with C = 1 cannot have four phases coexisting in equilibrium.

Maximum phases for C = 1: P = 3 (gives F = 0 — triple point, just possible).
P = 4 would require F = –1 which has no physical meaning — it's thermodynamically impossible.

For a two-component system (C = 2): Maximum phases = 4 (gives F = 0 — quadruple point).
10Why is rhombic sulphur more common in nature than monoclinic sulphur?▼
Answer

This is because of the thermodynamic stability of rhombic sulphur at ordinary (room) temperature and pressure.

  • Rhombic sulphur (α-S) is the stable form below 95.6°C at atmospheric pressure.
  • Monoclinic sulphur (β-S) is stable only between 95.6°C and 119°C.
  • Nature's average temperature is well below 95.6°C, so rhombic is the thermodynamically stable allotrope.
  • If monoclinic sulphur is cooled below 95.6°C, it slowly converts back to rhombic sulphur (though this conversion can be slow — kinetics matter).
Summary: Since normal environmental temperatures are below the 95.6°C transition temperature, rhombic sulphur is the thermodynamically stable (lower free energy) allotrope and dominates in nature. Monoclinic sulphur converts back to rhombic on cooling.

12What are congruent and incongruent melting points? Give examples.▼
Answer

Congruent Melting Point: A compound melts without decomposition to give a liquid of the same composition as the solid. The compound is stable up to its melting point.

Example: KNO₃ melts congruently. CaCl₂·6H₂O melts congruently at 29.2°C. The liquid formed has the same ratio of components as the solid hydrate.

14What is a quadruple point?▼
Answer

A quadruple point is a point at which four phases of a two-component system (C = 2) coexist in equilibrium simultaneously.

Using Gibbs Phase Rule at quadruple point: F = C – P + 2 = 2 – 4 + 2 = 0 F = 0 → Invariant (completely fixed — one specific T, P, and composition)

Just like the triple point is the maximum for a one-component system (P = 3, F = 0), the quadruple point is the maximum for a two-component system (P = 4, F = 0).

Example: In the Na₂SO₄–H₂O system, a quadruple point occurs where Na₂SO₄·10H₂O (solid), Na₂SO₄ (anhydrous solid), saturated solution (liquid), and water vapour (gas) all coexist. This is at 32.4°C and a specific pressure.

A five-phase coexistence in a two-component system would require F = –1, which is impossible.

21Why does ice melt on heating but solid CO₂ converts directly to gas?▼
Answer

The answer lies in the triple point pressure of each substance:

PropertyWater (H₂O)Carbon dioxide (CO₂)
Triple point pressure4.58 mm Hg (0.006 atm) — BELOW 1 atm3880 mm Hg (5.11 atm) — ABOVE 1 atm
At 1 atm, what happens on heating?Solid (ice) → Liquid (water) → Gas (steam) — normal melting then boilingSolid CO₂ → directly Gas — sublimation (no liquid phase at 1 atm)
Explanation:
• For water: At 1 atm, we are above the triple point pressure (4.58 mm). So, as we heat ice, we cross the fusion curve (solid→liquid) before reaching the vaporisation curve. Ice melts first, then the liquid water eventually boils.

• For CO₂: At 1 atm, we are below the triple point pressure (5.11 atm). At this low pressure, there is no stable liquid phase. So, heating solid CO₂ at 1 atm takes it directly from solid to gas — it sublimes without passing through the liquid phase.
